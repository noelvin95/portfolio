My role

What I actually did here

I led the UX for this one — from the early research sessions right through to the screens that shipped. A lot of the job was the in-between work nobody sees: getting business and engineering into the same room, pulling apart what people said they wanted versus what they actually needed, and keeping the design honest across multiple squads who all had a stake in it. We ran it through IBM Design Thinking, so there was a steady rhythm of research, sketch, critique, repeat.

Research

Starting from a real gap, not a feature request

This didn't begin as “build a dashboard.” It began as a problem we kept hearing: the governance work was happening, but its value was invisible at the leadership level. A CDO could not, in one glance, answer “is our data in good shape?” — and that's the exact question they get asked in a board meeting.

So we spent real time with the people who'd live in this thing. What does a CDO actually care about? What's noise to them? Where do they currently go digging, and how painful is it? That shaped everything — the dashboard had to speak in outcomes a leader trusts, not in the raw plumbing the governance teams work with day to day.

“Is our data healthy?” sounds like a simple question. Giving someone a single, trustworthy place to answer it — that was the whole design problem.
Strategy

One view, but not one-size-fits-all

The big call early on was that this couldn't be a flat dashboard everyone sees the same way. A CDO, a data steward, and an engineer are looking for very different things. So we built it around role-based access — same source of truth, but the view reshapes itself around who's looking and what they're responsible for.

We also leaned into the AI side carefully. The point was never “let the AI decide” — it was to surface what needs attention and let the human stay in control. Same principle I bring to all my Data & AI work: the system proposes, the expert decides.

  • A clear top-level read on governance health — the thing a leader can show without translating it first.
  • Role-based views so each person lands on what's theirs, without drowning in everyone else's data.
  • AI-surfaced signals that point you to what's slipping, before it becomes a problem downstream.
Delivery

Getting it shipped, across squads

A lot of the craft here was coordination. This touched several squads, so a big part of my job was keeping the design coherent when many hands were on it — shared patterns, weekly critiques, and a lot of translating between “what the business asked for” and “what we can actually build well.”

It went live as part of IBM's Governance Insights public preview — so this isn't a concept sitting in a Figma file, it's running in production and in front of real customers.

Reflection

What I took from it

The thing I keep coming back to: the hardest part wasn't the visual design, it was getting everyone to agree on what “healthy” even means for a data estate. Once that was clear, the screens almost designed themselves. Good enterprise UX is mostly about earning that shared definition first — the pixels come after.
