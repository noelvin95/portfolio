IBM Knowledge Catalog · Case study 02

Bulk actions — stop doing the same thing fifty times

A data steward managing a governed catalogue often has to make the same change across a whole set of artifacts. But there was no way to select more than one and act on them together — every single update was a one-at-a-time grind. This was about giving them bulk actions, and getting them to value faster.

Who it's for

The data steward

The data steward

Owns the governed catalogue · day-to-day

They're the person responsible for keeping governance artifacts correct and up to date across the catalogue. When a policy shifts or a batch of terms needs the same edit, that work lands on them. Their day is full of small, repetitive changes — and at scale, “small and repetitive” quietly becomes the biggest time sink they have.

The problem

You could only ever touch one at a time

The catalogue simply had no bulk selection. If a steward needed to make the same change across ten artifacts, that was ten separate trips through the same flow — open it, edit it, save it, back out, do the next one. Nothing let them say “these five, all of them, this change.”

And it added up fast. Updating a single artifact took five clicks. Multiply that across a real catalogue and you've got a steward spending their afternoon clicking through near-identical screens — work that's not just slow, it's the kind of mind-numbing repetition where mistakes creep in.

When the same task is fast once but you have to do it fifty times, the design problem isn't the task — it's the fifty.
How I approached it

Make the set the unit of work, not the single item

The whole shift was reframing what a steward is acting on. Instead of “one artifact, then the next,” the design had to treat a selection as the thing you operate on. That sounds obvious, but in a governance tool it's loaded — you're letting someone change many governed things at once, so the design has to make the scope of what you're about to do completely clear, and forgiving if you get it wrong.

  • Clear multi-select that shows exactly how many artifacts are in play before you commit to anything.
  • One action applied confidently across the whole set, with the consequences visible up front.
  • Guardrails that fit governance — you should never be surprised by what a bulk action just did.
The result

From five clicks for one, to three for five

Reflection

What I took from it

This one's a good reminder that the most valuable design work isn't always a flashy new feature — sometimes it's removing friction from something people do constantly. Nobody asks for “bulk select” in a demo, but it's the thing that gives a steward their afternoon back. Designing for repetition, and for the moment something goes wrong at scale, is where the real care goes.
