IBM Knowledge Catalog · Case study 03

Custom artifacts — let people govern their own way

Every organisation governs its data a little differently. But the catalogue only shipped with a fixed set of prebuilt artifact types — if your governance model didn't fit those, you were stuck. This was about letting data stewards create their own custom governance artifacts, and making that feel easy rather than technical.

Who it's for

The data steward

The data steward

Owns the governed catalogue · day-to-day

They know their organisation's governance model better than anyone — the categories, the rules, the kinds of things that need tracking. But that knowledge had nowhere to go if it didn't match what the product already offered. They were forced to bend their real-world model to fit a fixed set of boxes.

The problem

You got the artifacts IBM shipped — and nothing else

The catalogue came with prebuilt artifact types only. There was no way for a steward to create their own. If an organisation's governance needed an artifact type that wasn't in the box, the answer was basically “sorry, you can't have that.”

For a governance product that's a real ceiling. Governance is specific to each business — the whole value is in modelling your rules accurately. Locking everyone to a fixed list meant the tool could never quite fit, and stewards had to work around it instead of with it.

A governance tool that only knows the artifacts it was born with can't grow with the organisation using it.
How I approached it

Powerful underneath, easy on the surface

The hard part of “let people create their own artifact type” is that it's inherently a structural, almost developer-flavoured task — you're defining a new kind of governed object. My job was to make that feel like something a steward could do confidently, without it turning into a technical chore.

So the design leaned on making the create flow guided and legible at every step — you always know what you're building, what it'll be used for, and what happens once it exists. Powerful capability, but shaped so the steward never feels like they've wandered into admin territory they don't understand.

  • A create flow that walks the steward through defining a new artifact type in plain terms.
  • Custom artifacts that behave like first-class citizens alongside the prebuilt ones — consistent, not bolted-on.
  • Enough structure to keep governance sound, without making the steward feel like they're configuring a system.
The result

From a fixed list to a model that fits

It shipped to production. Stewards went from being limited to whatever was prebuilt, to being able to create the governance artifacts their organisation actually needs — and the flow is easy enough that it doesn't need a specialist to drive it. The catalogue stopped dictating how you govern and started adapting to it.

Reflection

What I took from it

The interesting tension here was power versus simplicity. It would have been easy to ship something flexible but intimidating, or simple but useless. Getting a genuinely extensible capability to feel approachable — that's the line I spent the most time on, and it's the part I'm happiest with.
